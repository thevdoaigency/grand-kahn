The Parking Code Just Changed. Most Building Owners Haven't Noticed Yet.

July 2026
EV Charging

As of January 1, 2026, new construction in Illinois has to meet a number most property owners have never heard of: 40% of parking spaces have to be EV-capable. That threshold climbs to 50% in 2029, and 70% by 2034 (765 ILCS 1085/20, 1085/25). It's not a suggestion and it's not a rebate program you can opt into or skip — it's code.

That's the story right now. Not a national sales target for 2030. A parking requirement that's already in effect, in this state, on buildings breaking ground today.

What "EV-Capable" Actually Means

EV-capable doesn't mean a charger has to be installed and running on day one. It means the electrical panel capacity and conduit are already in place to support a Level 2 charger later, without tearing open a finished parking structure to add it. That distinction matters for budgeting: getting the rough-in right during construction is a fraction of the cost of retrofitting it after the fact. Get it wrong, or skip it, and the 2029 and 2034 thresholds turn into a much more expensive problem than they needed to be.

The Federal Picture Is Uncertain. The Local One Isn't.

Federal EV infrastructure funding has had a rough couple of years — NEVI funding was frozen in early 2025, restored later that year, then trimmed again in the 2026 federal budget. If you've been waiting for a clear signal out of Washington before you plan, you've been waiting for something that hasn't arrived.

Illinois hasn't waited. ComEd — which serves roughly 70% of the state — has paid out more than $100 million in EV-related rebates since 2024, funding over 7,200 charging ports and 2,200 fleet vehicles. And when federal support pulled back heading into 2026, ComEd didn't retreat with it — they raised their rebate amounts. That's not a talking point, that's the utility's own stated reasoning for the increase.

The state-level EV rebate program, ComEd's business and fleet rebates, and the new parking code are the three things actually worth planning around right now — not whatever happens next in Washington.

What This Means If You Own or Manage a Building

If you're breaking ground on new construction, the 40% threshold applies today, not someday. If you're managing an existing property, the math still matters: the buildings that plan panel capacity now are the ones that won't be scrambling — or paying retrofit premiums — when the 2029 and 2034 thresholds arrive. Multifamily, commercial, senior living, and institutional properties are all in scope, and the earlier the electrical planning happens, the cheaper it stays.
